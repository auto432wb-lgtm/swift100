RWA Map (Powered by rwa.xyz)

RWAs are real-world assets on-chain – things like US Treasuries, credit, real estate, and funds, tracked and settled on crypto rails.

  • Treasuries & Bills: Government debt paying a “risk-free” yield. On-chain, these show up as tokenized T-bill funds and vaults.
  • Credit: Loans to businesses or institutions. On-chain private credit = higher yield, but with real default risk.
  • Real Estate: Property exposure via tokens instead of traditional REIT shares.
  • Funds & Products: Wrappers that hold baskets of assets (bond funds, yield products, etc.).

Layer 1 vs Layer 2 (Base Chains vs Scaling Layers)

Blockchains stack like layers. Layer 1 is the base chain. Layer 2 is a scaling layer built on top of an L1.

Layer 1 (L1)

The main blockchain where blocks are produced and security lives. Apps, tokens, and NFTs are built directly on L1.

  • Examples: Bitcoin (BTC), Ethereum (ETH), Solana (SOL), Sui (SUI), Avalanche (AVAX), NEAR.
  • Role: Settlement layer, base security, “the main city.”
  • Trade-off: More secure / decentralized, but often slower and more expensive.

Layer 2 (L2)

A scaling layer that runs on top of an L1. It processes many transactions cheaply and periodically settles back to the base chain.

  • Examples: Arbitrum (ARB), Optimism (OP), Polygon (POL ecosystem), zkSync (ZK), Starknet (STRK), Base.
  • Role: “Express lanes” for cheaper, faster transactions.
  • Trade-off: Depends on the underlying L1 for security and final settlement.

When you see “Smart Contract L1” or “Ethereum L2” in Swift 100, this is the distinction it’s pointing at.

What Is DeFi?

DeFi (Decentralized Finance) is finance run by code instead of companies. Smart contracts hold funds and enforce rules, so anyone with a wallet can lend, borrow, trade, or earn yield.

Core DeFi Categories

  • Lending & Borrowing: Protocols like Aave (AAVE) and Compound (COMP) let you supply assets to earn yield or borrow against collateral.
  • DEXs (Decentralized Exchanges): Uniswap (UNI), Sushi (SUSHI) and others let you swap tokens without a centralized exchange.
  • Perps & Derivatives: GMX, dYdX (DYDX), Injective (INJ), Hyperliquid (HYPE) offer leveraged trading via smart contracts.
  • Stablecoins & Yield: Maker (MKR), Frax (FXS), Pendle (PENDLE) handle stablecoins and yield tokenization.

Why It Matters

  • Open access: No bank account needed. Just a wallet.
  • Transparency: Positions and rules are on-chain.
  • Programmability: Apps can plug into each other like Lego.

Key Risks

  • Smart contract bugs or exploits.
  • Oracle failures or bad pricing data.
  • Governance and token incentives breaking the economic model.

In the Swift 100 table, any asset tagged as DeFi Lending, DEX, Perps, or Stablecoin DeFi sits inside this DeFi stack.

Staking, Liquid Staking, and Restaking

Staking means locking your tokens to help secure a network. In return, you earn rewards (new tokens or a share of fees).

Staking

  • Examples: Staking ETH, SOL, AVAX, NEAR to support validators.
  • What you earn: Protocol rewards and/or transaction fees.
  • Main risks: Token price dropping, and in some systems validator slashing.

Liquid Staking

With liquid staking, you stake but receive a liquid “receipt token” (like stETH) that you can still use in DeFi.

  • Examples: Lido (LDO), Rocket Pool, Jito (JTO) on Solana.
  • Upside: You earn staking rewards and can still use the asset in other protocols.
  • Extra risks: Smart contract risk and potential de-pegging of the liquid staking token.

Restaking

Restaking reuses the security of staked assets for other protocols. Your stake secures multiple services at once.

  • Examples: EigenLayer (EIGEN), ether.fi (ETHFI) and related restaking projects.
  • Upside: Extra yield on top of normal staking rewards.
  • Extra risks: More complex smart contract risk and the chance that one failure impacts multiple layers.

In Swift 100, tokens tagged as Liquid Staking, Staking Infra, or Restaking are all parts of this staking stack.

What Is Decentralized Social?

Decentralized social (DeSo) is social media that runs on crypto rails instead of a single company’s database.

  • On-chain identity: Your profile and social graph (followers / following) are tied to your wallet, not a username on one app.
  • Open data: Multiple apps can build on the same social graph, so you can switch frontends without losing your audience.
  • Built-in monetization: Tips, NFTs, and tokens can be wired directly into the protocol.

In practice, this means a future where you own your account and can move your followers between apps, instead of starting from zero every time a new platform wins.

Some Swift 100 assets will plug into this indirectly (through messaging, identity, or infra), even if they are not pure “social tokens” on the surface.
